The New York Times crossword has fairly well-defined (though partly informal) construction standards. These aren’t all officially published in one place, but they’re consistently enforced by editors like Will Shortz and widely followed by constructors.

Here’s a structured breakdown:

---

# 🧩 Core NYT Crossword Construction Guidelines

## 1. Grid Structure

### Daily puzzles (Mon–Sat)

![Image](https://i.sstatic.net/tpPif.png)

![Image](https://www.crosswordsite.com/cdn/shop/products/us_15x15_puzzle_no_329_large.png?v=1556191579)

![Image](https://www.crosswordsite.com/cdn/shop/products/us_11x11_puzzle_no_342_large.png?v=1548939979)

* **Size:** 15×15
* **Symmetry:** Almost always **180° rotational symmetry**
* **Black squares:**

  * Typically **~15–17%** of the grid
  * Must be **symmetrically placed**
* **Connectivity:**

  * All white squares must be connected (no isolated sections)

### Sunday puzzles

* **Size:** 21×21
* Same rules, just larger

## 2. Theme (especially Mon–Thu, Sunday)

![Image](https://crosswordr.com/b4ffb8524b283da1de33.png)

![Image](https://images.wordmint.com/p/Unit_6_Transformations__Symmetry_658448.png)

![Image](https://tendency-prod.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/up09p59hhucso1op9jw8crc28bc5)

![Image](https://tendency-prod.nyc3.cdn.digitaloceanspaces.com/henth11dp9h7tue9f8aov6pevyad)

* Most puzzles have a **theme** (except many Fridays/Saturdays)
* Theme entries:

  * Usually **3–5 long answers**
  * Placed **symmetrically**
* Theme types:

  * Wordplay (puns, letter changes)
  * Rebus (multiple letters in one square)
  * Gimmicks (missing letters, visual tricks)

👉 Key rule:
**Theme answers must be consistent and clearly related**

---

## 3. Fill Quality

* **No obscurity overload**

  * Avoid rare words, abbreviations, or crosswordese unless necessary
* **No partial phrases**

  * Bad: “IN A ___”
* **Prefer lively, modern language**

  * Names, phrases, pop culture (balanced with timeless knowledge)

👉 Editors strongly prefer:

* Natural-sounding phrases
* Clean crossings (every letter confirmed by crossing answers)

---

## 4. Word Length & Structure

* Minimum answer length:

  * **3 letters**
* Avoid:

  * Too many short words (3–4 letters)
* Good puzzles:

  * Have a mix of lengths
  * Feature **long, interesting entries**

---

## 5. Crossings

* Every answer must be **cross-checked**
* Avoid:

  * Two obscure answers crossing each other
* Ideal:

  * If one answer is hard, the crossing answers are easy

---

## 6. Clue Style (by day)

| Day               | Difficulty    | Style                  |
| ----------------- | ------------- | ---------------------- |
| Monday            | Easy          | Straightforward        |
| Tuesday–Wednesday | Medium        | Some wordplay          |
| Thursday          | Trick/gimmick | Rebus, misdirection    |
| Friday–Saturday   | Hard          | No theme, clever clues |
| Sunday            | Medium        | Themed, longer solve   |

👉 Clues must be:

* Fair (no guessing required without logic)
* Grammatically consistent with the answer

---

## 7. No Duplication / Consistency

* No repeated answers
* Avoid repeating unusual words
* Maintain consistent tense, number, and style

---

## 8. Editorial Preferences

From Will Shortz’s era:

* Freshness matters:

  * Avoid tired fill like **ETUI, OREO, EPEE** (unless necessary)
* Inclusivity:

  * Broader cultural references than older puzzles
* Clean grid:

  * Minimize “junk fill”

---

# 🧠 A Few “Unwritten” Rules

Experienced constructors also follow:

* **“Monday should teach, Saturday should challenge”**
* **Theme density matters** (don’t force weak theme entries)
* **Grid design comes first, fill comes second**

---

# ⚠️ Common Reasons NYT Rejects a Puzzle

* Weak or inconsistent theme
* Too much crosswordese
* Ugly fill caused by forced theme placement
* Lack of sparkle (nothing memorable)

---

# If You’re Building Your Own (like your crossword project)

Given your interest in grid algorithms, the NYT rules translate nicely into constraints:

* Enforce **rotational symmetry**
* Optimize for **crossability (high intersection density)**
* Penalize:

  * Short words
  * Low-frequency vocabulary
* Ensure **single connected component** (your interlock work 👍)
